\begin{abstract}
\noindent Modern Extract--Transform--Load (ETL) pipelines are frequently invalidated by upstream schema evolution, leading to high manual maintenance costs and data downtime. This thesis addresses the central research question: To what extent can Retrieval-Augmented Large Language Models (RAG-LLMs) automate the detection of schema changes and the subsequent regeneration of valid transformation logic? To answer this, a three-stage methodology was developed and implemented: (i) a RAG-based LLM-assisted schema change detection mechanism, (ii) a RAG-based retrieval strategy that assembles task-relevant evidence from historical schemas and validated mappings, and (iii) an LLM-assisted regeneration component. The approach was evaluated using the Open Source NYC TLC Trip Record data across multiple evolution scenarios.

\noindent The findings reveal that suggested workflow achieved a cell-level mapping agreement of 98\% and decreased the time-to-adaptation from hours of manual engineering to an average of 86.5 seconds. The thesis also revealed some limitations like the stochastic character of smaller models like Mistral and unable to address column deletions accurately. The thesis shows that semantic integrity still requires a "human-in-the-loop" inspection although RAG-LLMs speed up ET maintenance. This work offers a scalable architecture for self-adapting data pipelines.
\end{abstract}
